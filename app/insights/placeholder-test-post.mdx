## The model is the easy part

Most teams reach for the model first. They pick GPT-4 or Claude or Llama, write a prompt, and ship something that looks like AI. Three weeks later, it's silently regressing in production and nobody has the data to prove it.

The hard parts of an AI product are downstream of the model:

- The eval suite that tells you whether the output is right
- The data pipeline that feeds it real, current information
- The deployment story when the model provider deprecates a checkpoint
- The UX that lets a non-engineer trust what it produced

We treat all four as first-class engineering work, not as scope creep. The model is the easy part — and increasingly, it's also the commoditized part.

## What an eval suite actually looks like

Here is the minimum we ship on every Build engagement:

```typescript
interface EvalCase {
  input: string;
  expected: { mustContain?: string[]; mustNotContain?: string[] };
  category: 'happy-path' | 'edge' | 'adversarial';
}

const cases: EvalCase[] = [
  // ~50 cases hand-curated from real user transcripts,
  // grouped across categories so regressions surface fast.
];
```

Every PR runs the suite. Every release is gated on its score. When the score drops, we stop and find out why before the next change.

> Evals are the product, not a checkpoint. The score is the contract for what *done* means.

## The build order that follows from this

If the model is the easy part and the eval is the contract, the natural build order is:

1. **Real data access** in week 1 — synthetic data tells you nothing useful
2. **Eval suite** by end of week 2 — at least 30 hand-curated cases
3. **Baseline model** by end of week 3 — the simplest thing that could work
4. **Iterate against the eval** for the rest of the engagement

The team that builds this way ships AI products that improve in production. The team that doesn't ships something demo-ready and watches it rot.
