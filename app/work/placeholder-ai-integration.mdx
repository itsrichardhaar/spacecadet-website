## The problem

The platform's triage team was spending most of every day classifying incoming patient inquiries by urgency. They had been paying a vendor for a classification API that needed manual review on roughly half its outputs. The team was effectively the eval suite for a black-box vendor model, and the vendor would not share their training data, evaluation methodology, or roadmap.

When HIPAA compliance review flagged the vendor's data-residency story as a risk, the team had a hard deadline to replace it.

## What we built

A drop-in classifier service embedded inside the platform's existing FastAPI backend. It reads from the same Postgres tables the triage team already uses, classifies incoming inquiries against a 14-category taxonomy, and surfaces a confidence score that drives the team's review queue.

- Data never leaves the platform's own infrastructure
- Owned eval suite of ~200 historical inquiries, classified by senior triage staff
- Confidence threshold tuned per category so high-stakes categories route to human review
- Telemetry dashboard for the team's clinical director to monitor classification drift

## Approach

1. **Week 1 — Discovery + data access.** Set up read access to the platform's Postgres in a HIPAA-compliant sandbox. No synthetic data — real inquiries from week 1.
2. **Week 2 — Eval suite.** Senior triage staff classified 200 historical inquiries. We froze that as the source of truth and never re-labelled it.
3. **Weeks 3–5 — Build.** Iterated against the eval suite. Every model change had to score at least as well as the previous version on the high-stakes categories.
4. **Weeks 6–7 — Integration.** Replaced the vendor API in the existing classification flow. Ran in shadow for a week, with the team comparing outputs side-by-side.
5. **Week 8 — Cutover + handoff.** Retired the vendor subscription. Two-week support window for monitoring drift and tuning thresholds.

## Outcome

The triage team got 62 hours per week back — time they redirected toward higher-acuity case review. The classification eval has held above 94% for the four months since launch; the team owns the eval suite and the model code, and they have a clear path to swap the underlying model when a better one ships.
